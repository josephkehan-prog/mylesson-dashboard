Starting in Spring 2013, \nNew York State began administering tests designed to assess student performance in accordance with the \ninstructional shifts and the rigor demanded by the Common Core State Standards (CCSS). To aid in the \ntransition to new assessments, New York State has released a number of resources, including test blueprints \nand specifications, sample questions, and criteria for writing assessment questions. These resources can be \nfound at http://www.engageny.org/common-core-assessments. \nNew York State administered the ELA/Literacy and Mathematics Common Core tests in April 2014 and is now \nmaking a portion of the questions from those tests available for review and use. These released questions will \nhelp students, families, educators, and the public better understand how tests have changed to assess the \ninstructional shifts demanded by the Common Core and to assess the rigor required to ensure that all students \nare on track to college and career readiness.\nAnnotated Questions Are Teaching Tools\nThe released questions are intended to help educators, students, families, and the public understand how the \nCommon Core is different. The annotated questions demonstrate the way the Common Core should drive \ninstruction and how tests have changed to better assess student performance in accordance with the \ninstructional shifts demanded by the Common Core. They are also intended to help educators identify how \nthe rigor of the State tests can inform classroom instruction and local assessment. To this end, these annotated \nquestions will include instructional suggestions for mastery of the CCLS. \nThe annotated questions will include both multiple-choice and constructed-response questions. With each \nmultiple-choice question released, a rationale will be available to demonstrate why the question measures \nthe intended standards; why the correct answer is correct; and why each wrong answer is plausible but \nincorrect. Additionally, for each constructed-response question, there will be an explanation for why the \nquestion measures the intended standards and an annotated rubric with sample student responses that \nwould obtain each score on the rubric. \nUnderstanding ELA Annotated Questions\nMultiple Choice\nMultiple-choice questions are designed to assess Common Core Reading and Language Standards. They will \nask students to analyze different aspects of a given text, including central idea, style elements, character and \nplot development, and vocabulary. Almost all questions, including vocabulary questions, will only be \nanswered correctly if the student comprehends and makes use of the whole passage. For multiple-choice \nquestions, students will select the correct response from four answer choices. \nMultiple-choice questions will assess Reading Standards in a range of ways. Some will ask students to analyze \naspects of text or vocabulary. Many questions will require students to combine skills. For example, questions \nmay ask students to identify a segment of text that best supports the central idea. To answer correctly, a \nstudent must first comprehend the central idea and then show understanding of how that idea is supported. \nQuestions will require more than rote recall or identification. Students will also be required to negotiate \nplausible, text-based distractors1. Each distractor will require students to comprehend the whole passage.\nThe rationales describe why the distractors are plausible but incorrect and are based in common \nmisconceptions regarding the text. While these rationales will speak to a possible and likely reason for \nselection of the incorrect option by the student, these rationales do not contain definitive statements as to why \nthe student chose the incorrect option or what we can infer about knowledge and skills of the student based \non their selection of an incorrect response. These multiple-choice questions were designed to assess student \nproficiency, not to diagnose specific misconceptions/errors with each and every incorrect option.\nThe annotations accompanying the multiple-choice questions will also include instructional suggestions for \nmastery of the CCLS measured.\nShort Response\nShort-response questions are designed to assess Common Core Reading and Language Standards. These are \nsingle questions in which students use textual evidence to support their own answer to an inferential question. \nThese questions ask the student to make an inference (a claim, position, or conclusion) based on his or her \nanalysis of the passage, and then provide two pieces of text-based evidence to support his or her answer. \nThe purpose of the short-response questions is to assess a student’s ability to comprehend and analyze text. In \nresponding to these questions, students will be expected to write in complete sentences. Responses should \nrequire no more than three complete sentences\nThe rubric used for evaluating short-response questions can be found both in the grade-level annotations and \nin the Educator Guide to the 2014 Grade 3 Common Core English Language Arts Test at \nwww.engageny.org/resource/test-guides-for-english-language-arts-and-mathematics.\nExtended Response\nExtended-response questions are designed to measure a student’s ability to Write from Sources. Questions \nthat measure Writing from Sources prompt students to communicate a clear and coherent analysis of one or \ntwo texts. The comprehension and analysis required by each extended response is directly related to grade \nspecific reading standards.\nStudent responses are evaluated on the degree to which they meet grade-level writing and language \nexpectations. This evaluation is made using a rubric that incorporates the demands of grade specific Common \nCore Writing, Reading, and Language standards. The integrated nature of the Common Core Learning \nStandards for ELA and Literacy require that students are evaluated across the strands (Reading, Writing, and \nLanguage) with longer piece of writing such as those prompted by the extended-response questions. \nThe information in the annotated extended-responses questions focuses on the demands of the questions and \nas such will show how the question measures the Common Core Reading standards. \nThe rubric used for evaluating extended-response can be found both in the grade-level annotations and in the \nEducator Guide to the 2014 Grade 3 Common Core English Language Arts Test at \nwww.engageny.org/resource/test-guides-for-english-language-arts-and-mathematics.\nThese Released Questions Do Not Comprise a Mini Test\n1 A distractor is an incorrect response that may appear to be a plausible correct response to a student who \nhas not mastered the skill or concept being tested.\nThis document is NOT intended to show how operational tests look or to provide information about how \nteachers should administer the test; rather, its purpose is to provide an overview of how the new test reflects \nthe demand of the CCSS. \nThe released questions do not represent the full spectrum of standards assessed on the State tests, nor do they \nrepresent the full spectrum of how the Common Core should be taught and assessed in the classroom. \nSpecific criteria for writing test questions as well as additional assessment information is available at \nwww.engageny.org/common-core-assessments.\n203014P\nRead this article.

Purpose of the Study\nNWEA® is committed to providing partners with useful tools to help make inferences about \nstudent learning from MAP® Growth™ test scores. One important use of MAP Growth results is \nto predict a student’s performance on the state summative assessment at different times \nthroughout the year. This allows educators and parents to determine if a student is on track in \ntheir learning to meet state standards by the end of the year or, given a student’s learning \nprofile, is on track to obtain rigorous, realistic growth in their content knowledge and skills.\nThis document presents results from a linking study conducted by NWEA in July 2020 to \nstatistically connect the scores of the New York State Testing Program (NYSTP) in Grades 3–8 \nEnglish Language Arts (ELA) and Mathematics with Rasch Unit (RIT) scores from the MAP \nGrowth assessments taken during the Spring 2018 term. The linking study has been updated \nsince the previous version published in January 2020 to incorporate the new 2020 NWEA MAP \nGrowth norms (Thum & Kuhfeld, 2020). In this updated study, MAP Growth cut scores are also \nincluded for Grade 2 so educators can track early learners’ progress toward proficiency on the \nNYSTP test by Grade 3. This report presents the following results:\n1. Student sample demographics\n2. Descriptive statistics of test scores\n3. MAP Growth RIT cut scores that correspond to the NYSTP performance levels using the \nequipercentile linking procedure for the spring results and the 2020 norms for the fall and \nwinter results\n4. Classification accuracy statistics to determine the degree to which MAP Growth \naccurately predicts student proficiency status on the NYSTP tests\n5. The probability of achieving grade-level proficiency on the NYSTP assessment based on \nMAP Growth RIT scores from fall, winter, and spring using the 2020 norms\n1.2. Assessment Overview\nThe NYSTP Grades 3–8 ELA and Mathematics tests are New York State’s operational \nsummative assessments aligned to the New York State Learning Standards. Each assessment \nhas three cut scores (i.e., the minimum score a student must get on a test to be placed in a \ncertain performance level) that distinguish between the following performance levels: Level I, \nLevel II, Level III, and Level IV. The Level III cut score demarks the minimum level of \nperformance considered to be proficient for accountability purposes.\nMAP Growth interim assessments from NWEA are computer adaptive and aligned to state\u0002specific content standards. Scores are reported on the RIT vertical scale with a range of 100–\n350. Each content area has its own scale. To aid the interpretation of scores, NWEA periodically \nconducts norming studies of student and school performance on MAP Growth. Achievement \nstatus norms show how well a student performed on the MAP Growth test compared to students \nin the norming group by associating the student’s performance on the MAP Growth test, \nexpressed as a RIT score, with a percentile ranking. Growth norms provide expected score \ngains across test administrations (e.g., the relative evaluation of a student’s growth from fall to \nspring). The most recent norms study was conducted in 2020 (Thum & Kuhfeld, 2020).\nLinking Study: Predicting Performance on New York NYSTP from MAP Growth Page 8\n2. Methods\n2.1. Data Collection\nThis linking study is based on data from the Spring 2018 administrations of the MAP Growth \nand NYSTP assessments. NWEA recruited New York districts to participate in the study by \nsharing their student and score data for the target term. Districts also gave NWEA permission to \naccess students’ associated MAP Growth scores from the NWEA in-house database. Once \nNew York state score information was received by NWEA, each student’s state testing record \nwas matched to their MAP Growth score by using the student’s first and last names, date of \nbirth, student ID, and other available identifying information. Only students who took both the \nMAP Growth and NYSTP assessments in Spring 2018 were included in the study sample.\n2.2. Post-Stratification Weighting\nPost-stratification weights were applied to the calculations to ensure that the linking study \nsample represented the state population in terms of race, sex, and performance level. These \nvariables were selected because they are correlated with the student’s academic achievement \nwithin this study and are often provided in the data for the state population. The weighted\nsample matches the target population as closely as possible on the key demographics and test \nscore characteristics. Specifically, a raking procedure was used to calculate the post\u0002stratification weights and improve the representativeness of the sample. Raking uses iterative \nprocedures to obtain weights that match sample marginal distributions to known population \nmargins. The following steps were taken during this process:\n• Calculate marginal distributions of race, sex, and performance level for the sample and \npopulation.\n• Calculate post-stratification weights with the rake function from the survey package in R\n(Lumley, 2019).\n• Trim the weight if it is not in the range of 0.3 to 3.0.\n• Apply the weights to the sample before conducting the linking study analyses.\n2.3. MAP Growth Cut Scores\nThe equipercentile linking method (Kolen & Brennan, 2004) was used to identify the spring MAP \nGrowth RIT scores that correspond to the spring NYSTP performance level cut scores. Spring \ncuts for Grade 2 were derived based on the cuts for Grade 3 and the 2020 NWEA growth \nnorms. RIT fall and winter cut scores that predict proficiency on the spring NYSTP test were \nthen projected using the 2020 growth norms. Percentile ranks are also provided that show how \na nationally representative sample of students in the same grade scored on MAP Growth for \neach administration, which is an important interpretation of RIT scores. This is useful for \nunderstanding (1) how student scores compare to peers nationwide and (2) the relative rigor of \na state’s performance level designations for its summative assessment.\nThe MAP Growth spring cut scores for Grades 3–8 could be calculated using the equipercentile \nlinking method because that data are directly connected to the NYSTP spring data used in the \nstudy. The equipercentile linking procedure matches scores on the two scales that have the \nsame percentile rank (i.e., the proportion of tests at or below each score). For example, let 𝑥\nrepresent a score on Test 𝑋 (e.g., NYSTP). Polynomial loglinear pre-smoothing was applied to reduce irregularities of the score \ndistributions and equipercentile linking curve.\nThe MAP Growth conditional growth norms provide students’ expected score gains across \nterms, such as growth from fall or winter to spring within the same grade or from spring of a \nlower grade to the spring of the adjacent higher grade. This information can be used to calculate \nthe fall and winter cut scores for Grades 3–8 and the fall, winter, and spring cut scores for \nGrade 2. The calculation of fall \nand winter cuts for Grade 2 followed the same process as the other grades. For example, the \ngrowth score from fall to spring in Grade 2 was used to calculate the fall cuts for Grade 2.\n2.4. Modernizr.load({ test: Modernizr.input.placeholder, nope: [ '/blog/app/themes/rebrand-substance/assets/css/placeholder_polyfill.min.css', '/blog/app/themes/rebrand-substance/assets/js/lib/placeholder_polyfill.jquery.min.combo.js' ] }); \n\n \n\nMs. Ramírez has five minutes, a MAP® Growth™ Student Profile report, and a pressing question: What does Miguel need next?\n\nMiguel, a fifth grader, participates eagerly in class discussions and proudly turns in (most) assignments, yet his recent assessment results surprised her. As she reviews his instructional area strengths and suggested focus skills, the data begins pointing toward instructional decisions that put Miguel—the learner, not the score—at the center.\n\nWe live in a world full of data, and schools are no exception. But data literacy doesn’t develop by osmosis, and teachers don’t need more click paths; we need practical ways to interpret what we’re seeing and translate it into action. Think of the MAP Growth Student Profile report not as a technical document but as a set of lenses you can use to understand each learner more clearly.\n\nIn a recent article, we explored how the Class Profile report helps teachers see patterns across a group. Here we zoom in from class trends to individual students. By the end of this article, you’ll be able to:\n\n- Frame the Student Profile report in clear “zones” and guiding questions\n- Interpret what each section is designed to answer\n- Avoid common misconceptions\n- Use quick, practical teacher moves to turn insights into next steps\n\nLet’s start by walking through the report as a series of student-centered questions, because isn’t it time we put the “student” back in the Student Profile report?\n\n## Getting your bearings: The main “zones” of the Student Profile report\n\nOnce you open a MAP Growth Student Profile report, you’ll see four major sections that each answer a different question:\n\n1. Comparisons. “Am I okay?” “Where am I right now compared to peers?”\n2. Instructional areas. “What am I good at?” “Where can I grow?”\n3. Growth goal. “Where am I going next?”\n4. Growth over time. “How far have I come?”\n\nThink of these like panels on a dashboard. Each tells a different part of the student’s learning story—and none should be read alone.\n\n## Zone 1: Comparisons: “Am I okay?” “Where am I right now compared to peers?”\n\nLet’s start with the section that often carries the most emotional weight: comparisons.\n\nEven when no one says it out loud, this is the section that can trigger the quiet worries: “Am I behind?” “Did I fail?” “Is this bad?” ”I didn’t do as well as my friends.” This is where careful framing—and reframing of the word “comparison”—matters.\n\nThis zone shows how a student’s current achievement compares to other students who took the same assessment. It includes percentile information and achievement context. A helpful analogy is the pediatrician’s height and weight chart. When a child is measured, the doctor plots today’s numbers relative to other children the same age. It’s not a judgment; it’s a reference point. This data point guides next steps; it doesn’t define identity. The comparisons section of the Student Profile report is similar. It says, “Here’s where you are right now, compared to a group of students similar to you, on this day, so we can decide where to go next.”\n\n## Common misconceptions to address\n\n- Percentile ≠ intelligence (or “percent correct”). “Percentile” reflects relative standing, not how “smart” a student is or how many items they answered correctly.\n- Below average ≠ failure. “Below average” means fewer students scored at or below this level at this moment in time.\n- Above average ≠ “finished growing.” “Above average” doesn’t mean a student is done growing. Every learner has next steps.\n- One score ≠ the full story. The assessment was one hour of one day, and a single score doesn’t paint a complete picture. Classroom evidence, student work, and formative assessment still matter.\n\n## A one-minute teacher move\n\nTry this language with students: “This is a temperature check, not a pass/fail result. It helps us see where you are right now so we can plan what’s next. I also use your classwork and projects to understand your learning. Let’s name one strength to leverage and one focus area to explore.” This keeps dignity intact and direction clear.\n\n## Zone 2: Instructional areas: “What am I good at?” “Where can I grow?”\n\nThis section of the MAP Growth Student Profile report breaks performance into skill-based areas. It turns a general statement like “You’re strong in math” into something more instructionally useful: “You’re strong at identifying and comparing shapes” or “You’re ready to strengthen fraction equivalence.”\n\nThis is where the report becomes especially actionable. Instead of a single RIT score, you get skill-level clues that can inform grouping, scaffolds, and stretch opportunities. This zone is your treasure chest for small-group formation(“Who needs similar exposure to skills?”); tutoring and WIN time focus (“What can we hit in 30 minutes?”); intervention planning(“What’s something they need, right now?”); and student goal-setting conversations (“How can we get you more practice with this skill?”).\n\nThere aren’t enough hours in the day to just get “more of everything.” This zone helps us understand which specific skills a student should target. It provides smarter focus.\n\n## Common misconceptions to address\n\n- Green ≠ being fully ready for everything next. Intentional review, increased depth of existing concepts, and consistent skills application are essential. It’s important to prioritize rigor, and instruction will be more successful if we understand what a student is ready for.\n- Red ≠ contradiction of good grades. A RIT score doesn’t override grades. Classroom grades can reflect effort, completion, and participation alongside mastery. That’s why triangulating data sources is a best practice.\n- Instructional areas ≠ exact lesson plans. Instructional areas are signals and categories, not a standards checklist or scripted curriculum.\n\n## A one-minute teacher move\n\nForm a just-in-time small group anchored to one foundational skill that appears on several students’ Student Profile report. Or, with a student, co-create two asset-based statements and one next step: “You analyze shapes well, and you use precise math language when you explain your work. Next, we’ll build fraction equivalence with visuals.”\n\n## Zone 3: Growth goal: “Where am I going next?”\n\nThe growth goal section of the MAP Growth Student Profile report gives a projected growth target. It’s a number but—more importantly—it’s a direction.\n\nIf a student sets a goal to run seven miles by June, the number matters, but the plan matters more. What practice, pacing, and checkpoints will help them get there? How will they feel if they only run six? Is that okay?\n\nA growth goal is not a verdict. It’s a forward-looking marker that helps teachers and students align effort and expectations.\n\n## Common misconceptions to address\n\n- Projection ≠ promise. Remember that a projection is a modeled path, not a guarantee.\n- Goal “setting” ≠ “set it and forget it.” Goals should be revisited and—most importantly— adjusted.\n- “Goal met” ≠ all done. It’s wonderful when a student meets a goal, but remember that learning continues with new depth and applications.\n- “Goal not met” ≠ failure. Missing a goal signals a need to recalibrate supports and strategies—and set a new goal.\n\n## A one-minute teacher move\n\nFrame it like this: “This is our training plan. We’ll check mile markers along the way and adjust if needed.” Work with students to define bite-sized milestones connected to real classwork so progress feels visible and attainable. I encourage you to use our downloadable forms “Student goal setting” and “Student goal setting: From X to Y, how and when?”\n\n## Zone 4: Growth over time: “How far have I come?”\n\nThis section appears in the MAP Growth Student Profile report when you have multiple test events. It shows a trend line across time.\n\nIf comparisons are today’s vitals, this is the training log. You wouldn’t judge a season of training by one workout! A log reveals patterns and payoffs. Growth-over-time data helps build academic identity around progress, not perfection—the entire point of the MAP Growth assessment.\n\nCommon misconceptions to address\n\n- One dip ≠ regression. Don’t let one dip throw everything off track. Look for trends, not single points.\n- A steep jump ≠ permanent mastery. When a student has a big leap in growth, celebrate! Then evaluate why the jump happened and implement a plan to help a student retain what they’ve learned.\n\n## A one-minute teacher move\n\nPrint out their growth chart, and invite students to annotate it with prompts like, “What do you notice?” “What have we tried that might have helped?” “What habit should we keep?” Then capture (and share out!) a one-sentence reflection: “Since fall, I’ve improved most in ___ by ___.”\n\nStudent self-assessment can be a game changer. Making time for it can support good habits and improve engagement. And when students share their goals aloud, it normalizes that we all have somewhere to go.\n\n## Bringing it back to the learner\n\nMAP Growth data doesn’t teach students; teachers do. But when the Student Profile report is used as a set of lenses—grounded in strengths, growth, and real instructional questions—it helps ensure the data serves the learner, not the other way around.\n\nMost importantly—and perhaps unsurprisingly—the MAP Growth Student Profile report is a tool that works best when you start not with the score, but with the student.\n\n \n\n \n\n \n\nVideo\n\n#### What does real student engagement with data look like?\n\nTeachers hear a lot about getting students involved with their test data and goal setting, but how does that happen in practice?

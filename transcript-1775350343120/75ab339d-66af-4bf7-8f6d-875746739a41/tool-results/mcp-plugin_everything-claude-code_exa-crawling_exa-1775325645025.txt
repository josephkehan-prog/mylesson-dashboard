Copies of the Rating Guides should be kept on file at the school for reference \nfor at least one year.\n2 2025 Scoring Leader Handbook\nOrganizing the Rating Process\nThe following is a suggested procedure for managing the mechanics of the rating process.\n1. Designate one person as the coordinator of the rating process. The coordinator may be \nappointed at either the building or the district level. The coordinator will be responsible for \ntraining and supervising raters and may also rate some of the questions in the student test \nbooklets.\n2. Set aside one room as a central rating room for collecting, sorting, circulating, and storing test \nbooklets and for preparing and maintaining records.\n3. Provide adequate time for rating during the school day.\n4. Make sure that each rater receives approximately the same number of student booklets, taking \ncare to ensure that no teacher is assigned to rate the test booklets of their own students.\n5. At least two teachers must participate in rating the open-ended questions. Each should be \nresponsible for rating a selected number of the open-ended questions in each test booklet. No \none teacher is to score more than half of all of the open-ended questions in a student’s test \nbooklet.\n6. Prior to the rating of students’ responses to the open-ended questions, allow time to provide \ntraining for all raters (even if they have received prior training). Please select, at random, 10–15 \nstudent test booklets and duplicate them for use as a “warmup” exercise with the raters for \ndiscussion of the questions, the scoring criteria, and the rating of each item. During the training \nexercise, after three or four complete student test booklets have been rated, ask the raters to \nindicate, by a show of hands, how many credits they gave to each response. If there is some \ndisagreement about the number of credits to be awarded to a response, ask raters who gave \ndifferent credits to a response to explain their rating by citing specific criteria from the Rating \nGuide.\nNote: A list of Ancillary Science Materials for the Scoring Site Coordinator can be found on page 12.\n2025 Scoring Leader Handbook 3\nImportant Information for Scoring the\nGrades 3–8 ELA and Mathematics Tests\nScoring Models\nAll tests can be scored regionally, districtwide, locally, or by a private contractor. Before scoring begins, the Scoring \nLeader(s) should meet with the Scoring Site Coordinator, who can familiarize the Scoring Leader(s) with \nthe scoring operations and scoring constructed responses. For each test administration, the Table Facilitator must \nalso meet current scoring standards. \nOne of the primary tasks of the Table Facilitator is to monitor the time the Scorers spend reading each \nstudent paper. If Scorers read too quickly, they may miss key information and incorrectly score a student \nresponse. To ensure efficiency, accuracy, and consistency in the scoring process, the Table Facilitator will \nrandomly select and score a few of each Scorer’s scored responses. This read-behind process allows the \nTable Facilitator to provide guidance during scoring by reinforcing the appropriate part of the rubric. \nIn addition, the Table Facilitator is required to complete the following tasks: \nl serve as the table’s primary resource for information on scoring rubrics and methods; if unsure of a \nscore point, the Table Facilitator will consult with the Scoring Leader for further clarification;\nl maintain a quiet atmosphere at the table by keeping talking and other distractions at a minimum;\nl perform read-behinds and record the results, number performed, and whether the Table Facilitator \nagreed with the assigned score (see the PBT Table Facilitators’ Record of Read-Behinds, \nAppendix F). Read-behinds are scored sets of test booklets that are \nread again as a check of Scorer accuracy and consistency.\nThe Table Facilitators conduct read-behinds on completed test booklets. Approximately 12 sets of test \nbooklets per hour (that is, two sets per Scorer) are read by the Table Facilitator.\nA Table Facilitator should not question a score if a response is a “fence-sitter” paper (i.e., a paper for \nwhich either of two contiguous scores could be justified). However, if the Table Facilitator believes that a \nparticular paper’s score is not in alignment with the rubrics or that an errant pattern of scoring has \ndeveloped (i.e., scores are continually out of alignment with the rubrics), the Table Facilitator should not \nchange the score, but should discuss the response(s) in question with the Scorer so that the Scorer is able \nto correct any scoring errors and return to accurate and consistent scoring.\nIf a problem persists, the Table Facilitator should bring the situation to the attention of the Scoring Site \nCoordinator.\nScorers\nScorers are teachers or other qualified school educators, representing Grades 3–8, who have been \ndesignated by school principals. Explain that the Training Set contains \nsample student responses that will be used as references (guide papers) when the Scorers begin to score \nactual student responses. Ask them not to read ahead, but rather to stay on the page being discussed.\nWhenever introducing material in the Training Set, inform the Scorers which page is being read aloud so \nthat they can read along silently. To ensure Scorers are viewing the correct page during training, the \nScoring Leader should identify the pages for the Scorers by referring to the identification information \nabove the student response (e.g., Guide Paper 1, CAS 1, etc.) along with the question number.
